Description

These HITS are  part of an ongoing research project that is attempting to analyze the arguments presented in online debates. Each pair of phrases below is taken from an online, public debate forum. The first phrase in the pair is from one author, and the second phrase is from another author. The two phrases may be from author's communicating directly with each other, or they may be from completely different discussions.

Instructions

We would like you to classify each of the following sets of pairs based on your perception of how SIMILAR the arguments are, on the following scale, examples follow.

(5) Completely equivalent, mean pretty much exactly the same thing, using different words.
(4) Mostly equivalent, but some unimportant details differ. One argument may be more specific than another or include a relatively unimportant extra fact.
(3) Roughly equivalent, but some important information differs/missing. This includes cases where the argument is about the same FACET but the authors have different stances on that facet.
(2) Not equivalent, but share some details. For example,  talking about the same entities but making different arguments (different facets)
(1) Not equivalent, but are on same topic
(0) On a different topic

Facet: A facet is a low level issue that often reoccurs in many arguments in support of the author's stance or in attacking the other author's position. There are many ways to argue for your stance on a topic. For example, in a discussion about the death penalty you may argue in favor of it by claiming that it deters crime. Alternatively, you may argue in favor of the death penalty because it gives victims of the crimes closure. On the other hand you may argue against the death penalty because some innocent people will be wrongfully executed or because it is a cruel and unusual punishment. Each of these specific points is a facet.

For two utterances to be about the same facet, it is not necessary that the authors have the same belief toward the facet. For example, one author may believe that the death penalty is a cruel and unusual punishment while the other one attacks that position. However, in order to attack that position they must be discussing the same facet.

Examples of 6 pairs with scores and explanations for each pair is shown below.

Sample 1
Score:  5   Completely equivalent, mean pretty much exactly the same thing

Two phrases are from the same topic, discuss same arguments/aspects, represent same side of the debate. None of the phrases conveys any additional information.
Sentence1: Gay marriage is unlikely to be recognized in future.  
Sentence2: Gay marriage is not legally recognized.

Here both the phrases talk about the same topic of gay marriage, arguing the same facet of recognition of gay marriage and opposing its recognition.

Sample 2
Score:  4   Mostly equivalent, but some unimportant details differ

Two phrases are from the same topic, discuss same facets, represent the same side of the debate. One phrase carries some additional information but the information is insignificant for that argument.
Sentence1: Broadened marriage is a side effect of legal gay marriage.  
Sentence2: definition of marriage should not be changed to include homosexual unions.

Both the phrases present an argument that Homosexual unions if legalized may change the definition of marriage.

Sample 3
Score: 3 Roughly equivalent, but some important information differs/missing, e.g. the facet is the same but the authors are contradicting each other.

  • Two phrases are from the same topic, discuss same arguments, but the phrases may contain some additional important information or a piece of evidence in support of that argument.
    Sentence1: In 1909, being homosexual was morally wrong.
    Sentence2: Homosexual acts were present at times.
    Here both the sentences convey the sentiment associated with homosexuality in earlier times, but second sentence also brings in the moral aspect.
     
  • Two phrases are from same topic , discuss the same facet  but one is in favor and other is against.
    Sentence1: gay community is the cause of the AIDs epidemic in the US.  
    Sentence2: statement of gays spreading AIDs is bigoted.
    This is an argument about the attribution of AIDS to gay community, however the two statements represent opposite sides of the debate.

Sample 4
Score: 2   Not equivalent, but share some details

Two phrases represent a part of the debate on the same broad topic such as gay marriage, but are on different aspects of gay marriage such as one on legal aspect and another on gay marriage in history.
Sentence1: Gay marriage is about health and other benefits for the partner, not about morality.  
Sentence2: Homosexual liaisons were regarded as unions for a particular purpose, rather than as marriage.

Here we infer that the discussion is about gay marriage. The arguments are different, but still there is some similarity among arguments as they both discuss the pros and cons of gay marriage, and the purpose of gay marriage. Note how these are more similar than Sample 5.

Sample 5
Score: 1 Not equivalent, but are on same topic

Sentence1: Equal marriage hurts heterosexuals.  
Sentence2: West Coast will have gay marriage laws before any other states.

The two phrases are a part of the same debate but with completely different argument facets and focus.
The argument in first sentence suggests that Gay marriage can hurt heterosexuals, the second argument talks about where Gay Marriage has more legal and popular support. 

Sample 6
Score:  0 On different topic

The two phrases are on completely different topics.
Sentence1: 20 large earthquakes in the latter half of this century, compared to about 15 for the first half and about 7 in the entire 19th century.  
Sentence2: You cannot justify an anti-gay agenda on morals or religion.

The first statement is about the earthquakes and second on homosexuals.
